# communication-patterns: Stakeholder Messaging Expertise

Advanced patterns for navigating complex organizational communication, especially with difficult stakeholders, governance messaging, and executive updates.

## When to Use This Skill

Invoke when:
- Drafting emails or messages for stakeholders
- Responding to pushback or resistance
- Handling non-responsive or evasive collaborators
- Crafting executive communications
- Need to maintain boundaries while reducing friction

## Communication Templates

### Weekly Status Format
**Structure:** Priorities → Completed → Working On (by workstream) → Blockers → Soon

### Executive Emails
**Structure:** Impact → data → ask. One page maximum.

### Messages (Teams/Slack)
**Pattern:** Direct update, tag stakeholders, include deadline, maintain thread context.

## Advanced Patterns for Difficult Stakeholders

### 1. Governance Routing Without Over-Explaining
**Pattern:** Acknowledge → Direct to owner → Copy them in

When routing access requests to appropriate owners, lead with the routing action rather than validating the request. Avoid explaining why access is restricted or providing too much context about policy changes.

**Why:** Over-explaining can encourage pushback or create the impression that restrictions are negotiable. Clean routing establishes the boundary without friction.

### 2. Non-Responsive Stakeholder Pattern
**Recognize:** When stakeholders respond to direct questions with their own questions ("Is this what you want?" "Do I have to...?"), they're negotiating whether they have to comply, not seeking clarification.

**Don't:** Re-answer or provide more examples — this validates the evasion and teaches them that resistance gets accommodation.

**Do:**
- Reframe their non-compliance as self-sabotaging: "Debating whether the questions are necessary only delays evaluation"
- Emphasize process universality: "Every request goes through the same review" removes personal dynamic
- End with constructive action: "Make a best effort to provide this" gives permission to be imperfect while requiring substance

**Why:** Difficult stakeholders test whether your requirements are negotiable. Answering questions about the questions signals flexibility. Redirecting to consequences and process removes the negotiation surface area.

### 3. Soften Confrontation While Maintaining Boundary
**Avoid:** "Questions about the questions" — confrontational and creates defensiveness

**Use:** "Debating whether the questions are necessary only delays evaluation" — achieves the same boundary without accusation

**Pattern:** Describe the behavior's consequence, not the behavior's character.

**Why:** Confrontational framing invites argument. Consequence framing invites problem-solving. Both establish the boundary, but one reduces friction.

### 4. Standard Process as Boundary-Setting Tool
When stakeholders push back on requirements, cite that "every request goes through the same review process using the same criteria."

**Benefits:**
- Depersonalizes the conflict — it's not you being difficult, it's the documented process
- Makes special treatment impossible to grant without appearing to violate fairness
- Shifts burden: they're asking for an exception to standard process

**Why:** Challenging stakeholders often frame requirements as personal preference to create negotiation space. Framing as universal process removes your discretion from the equation.

### 5. Reframe Non-Compliance as Self-Sabotage
**Instead of:** "You're not answering my questions" → **Use:** "The request hasn't been evaluated because the required questions have unanswered items"

**Pattern:** Make their resistance the obstacle to their goal, not your enforcement of requirements.

**Why:** People resist when they feel controlled. They comply when they see their own behavior blocking what they want.

### 6. Remove Examples When Stakeholders Are Evading
Providing detailed examples can become a template for minimal compliance.

**When stakeholders are genuinely confused:** Examples help

**When stakeholders are negotiating requirements:** Examples give them a floor to meet rather than a ceiling to aspire to

**Better approach for resisters:** Specify structure without providing sample answers.

## Message Formatting

**Use plain text** rather than bold markdown formatting for professional communications.

**Pattern that fails:**
- `**Read Time: 2 min**`
- `**Status Update**`

**Pattern that works:**
- `Read Time: 2 min`
- `Status Update`

**Why:** Bold formatting creates visual clutter. Plain text with proper spacing creates cleaner hierarchy and more professional tone.
